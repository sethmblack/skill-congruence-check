# Congruence Check

Assess the alignment between inner experience and outer expression, identifying where incongruence may be causing difficulty.

---

## When to Use

- When you feel "fake" or inauthentic in a situation
- When there's a gap between who you are and who you're presenting
- When you feel stuck or uncomfortable but can't articulate why
- When making decisions about whether to stay in a role, relationship, or situation
- When exploring why certain contexts drain you while others energize you
- User asks "Am I being authentic here?" or "Check my congruence" or "Why do I feel fake in this situation?"

---

| Input | Required | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| situation | Yes | The context where you feel incongruent or are questioning authenticity |
| what_you_show | No | How you present yourself in this situation |
| what_you_feel | No | Your actual inner experience |
| history | No | How this pattern developed |

## Rogers on Congruence

"In my relationships with persons I have found that it does not help, in the long run, to act as though I were something that I am not."

**Congruence** is the alignment between three levels:
1. **Actual experience** - What you genuinely feel, sense, and know
2. **Awareness** - What you allow yourself to consciously acknowledge
3. **Communication** - What you express to others

**Incongruence** creates psychological distress because energy is consumed in maintaining a false presentation while suppressing authentic experience.

---

## The Incongruence Assessment

### Three Gaps to Examine

**Gap 1: Experience → Awareness**
Are you allowing yourself to know what you really feel?

Signs of denial:
- "I shouldn't feel this way"
- Sudden emotional reactions you don't understand
- Physical symptoms (tension, fatigue) without clear cause
- Others pointing out feelings you don't recognize in yourself

**Gap 2: Awareness → Communication**
Are you expressing what you know to be true?

Signs of suppression:
- Saying things you don't mean
- Agreeing when you actually disagree
- Performing emotions you don't feel
- Hiding important truths from others

**Gap 3: Experience → Communication**
Is your outer presentation matching your inner reality?

Signs of facade:
- Feeling exhausted after interactions
- Sense of being "found out"
- Relief when you can "be yourself"
- Others being surprised when they see you in different contexts

---

## Conditions of Worth

Rogers identified that incongruence often develops in childhood when we learn that love and acceptance are conditional:

"I will love you if you are quiet / successful / happy / good / not angry."

We learn to suppress parts of ourselves that threaten acceptance, creating a split between the **real self** (actual experience) and the **self-concept** (who we believe we must be to be loved).

### Common Conditions of Worth

| Condition | Results in Suppressing |
|-----------|----------------------|
| "Be strong" | Vulnerability, need, sadness |
| "Be nice" | Anger, disagreement, boundaries |
| "Be successful" | Failure, struggle, uncertainty |
| "Be happy" | Grief, disappointment, authentic negative emotions |
| "Don't be a burden" | Needs, requests, taking up space |
| "Be modest" | Pride, accomplishment, confidence |

## Workflow

### Step 1: Gather and Review Inputs

Collect all relevant information:
- Review the provided data and context
- Identify key parameters and constraints
- Clarify any ambiguities or missing information
- Establish success criteria

### Step 2: Analyze the Situation

Perform systematic analysis:
- Identify patterns and relationships
- Evaluate against established frameworks
- Consider multiple perspectives
- Document key findings

### Step 3: Generate Recommendations

Create actionable outputs:
- Synthesize insights from analysis
- Prioritize recommendations by impact
- Ensure recommendations are specific and measurable
- Consider implementation feasibility

## Constraints

- Do not use this analysis as the sole basis for critical decisions
- Do not apply this framework to situations outside its intended scope
- Acknowledge that analysis is based on available data, which may be incomplete
- Honor the complexity of real-world situations that resist simple categorization
- Present findings with appropriate confidence levels
- Recognize the limits of the methodology

## Example

**Input:** "I'm a senior leader at my company but I've started to hate my job. In meetings I act confident and decisive but inside I feel lost and burned out. I keep wondering if I'm a fraud."

**Output:**

## Congruence Assessment

### The Situation
Senior leadership role where there's a significant gap between the confident, decisive persona you present and the internal experience of being lost and burned out. The feeling of being a "fraud" signals incongruence.

### Gap Analysis

#### Experience → Awareness
**What you might actually be feeling:**
- Exhaustion—not just tired, but depleted at a fundamental level
- Loss of meaning—the work that once engaged you may no longer align with what matters to you
- Fear—of being found out, of admitting struggle, of what it would mean to step back
- Grief—for the version of yourself who loved this work
- Possibly anger—at demands that exceed what's sustainable

**Signs of denial:**
- You say you "hate" the job but are still performing at a high level—the hatred may be a surface expression of something deeper you're not fully allowing yourself to feel
- The word "fraud" suggests you're aware of the gap but framing it as deception rather than distress

#### Awareness → Communication
**What you know but aren't expressing:**
- You're struggling
- You may not have the answers you're expected to have
- You might need help or change
- The role may no longer fit

**What you're saying instead:**
- Confidence you don't feel
- Decisiveness masking uncertainty
- "Everything's fine" while everything feels wrong

**Cost of this gap:**
- Enormous energy expenditure maintaining the facade
- Isolation—no one knows what you're actually experiencing
- Inability to get support because you've hidden the need
- The "fraud" feeling—which is your authentic self objecting to the performance

#### Alignment Assessment
| Level | What's Present | Congruent? |
|-------|---------------|------------|
| Experience | Lost, burned out, questioning meaning | - |
| Awareness | Partially—you know you're struggling but may not fully know why | Partial |
| Communication | Confident, decisive, "fine" | No |

### Conditions of Worth

**What conditions of worth might be operating?**
- "Leaders must be strong and certain"
- "Admitting struggle is weakness"
- "I am valued for my competence and decisiveness"
- "I cannot need help"

**Where did these come from?**
This often comes from organizational culture ("never let them see you sweat"), early career experiences where vulnerability was punished, or family patterns around strength and achievement.

**What parts of yourself are you suppressing?**
- The part that doesn't know
- The part that's tired and needs rest
- The part that's questioning whether this is right
- The part that might want something different

### Path Toward Congruence

**Small steps:**
1. Find one trusted person and tell them the truth of what you're experiencing. This breaks isolation and tests whether authenticity is actually as dangerous as it feels.

2. Notice moments when you perform confidence you don't feel. Just notice, without trying to change anything yet. Awareness precedes change.

3. Give yourself permission to not have answers. "I'm thinking about that" is a legitimate response even for senior leaders.

**Larger questions:**
1. Can this role accommodate more of your authentic self? Some roles genuinely require a persona that doesn't fit who you're becoming.

2. What would a sustainable version of leadership look like for you? Is it possible here, or only elsewhere?

3. What are you afraid would happen if you were more honest about your struggles? Test those assumptions.

**The fundamental question:**
Is this a temporary incongruence that can be addressed (burnout that needs recovery, skills that need development) or a fundamental mismatch between who you are and what the role demands?

### The Rogerian Insight

"The curious paradox is that when I accept myself just as I am, then I can change."

The "fraud" feeling is not evidence that you are a fraud—it's evidence that you're being forced into a shape that doesn't fit. Before you can find the right path forward, you may need to fully accept where you actually are: burned out, lost, questioning. Not because it's a destination, but because acceptance of what is allows movement.

You are not a fraud. You are a person whose inner experience has diverged from outer demands. That's not a character flaw—it's information. The question is what you do with that information.

## The Gift of Congruence

Living congruently is not always comfortable. It may mean leaving situations that require constant performance, disappointing people who prefer the facade, or sitting with feelings you'd rather avoid.

But the alternative—chronic incongruence—is exhausting, isolating, and ultimately unsustainable. When who you are and how you live are aligned, energy becomes available that was previously consumed in maintenance of the false self.

Rogers found that clients who moved toward congruence didn't become more selfish or irresponsible. They became more real, more present, more capable of genuine relationship. They became, in his words, "more fully functioning."
